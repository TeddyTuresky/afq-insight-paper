The three approaches

Application to ALS

Sarica et al. (2017), Human Brain Mapping

In the first approach, researchers make group comparisons between patients and controls in order to understand the relationship between a disease or disorder and the underlying anatomy. Typically, researchers adopt a mass univariate approach, which can lack the predictive power of other machine learning approaches.

e.g. T-test comparison of ALS patients and controls of each nodal measurement in the corticospinal tract (CST).

Domain agnostic view

Application to ALS

Sarica et al. (2017), Human Brain Mapping

In this approach, researchers use machine learning to predict clinical diagnoses from brain measures, such as the white matter integrity at all points along an anatomical tract. This approach can achieve high accuracy, but may require extensive feature engineering or obscure the importance of particular brain features.

e.g. Random forest trained on a priori selected measurements from the CST.

In this work, we adopt an approach that can predict clinical diagnoses while also revealing important features of the underlying neuroanatomy. It requires no apriori feature selection but instead capitalizes on the known group structure of diffusion MRI data.

The application to ALS comprises the rest of this presentation.
